NYC Transportation Analysis

Where — and when — New Yorkers ride

An end-to-end look at NYC's Citi Bike system: cleaning a month of raw trip data, then reading the patterns out of it two ways — when the system gets busy, and where — and writing up what it actually means. Built on January 2024's public trip data (~1.9M rides) and NYC DOT's bike lane network.

01 · The data

A month of trips, cleaned the same way three times

The trip data comes from Citi Bike's public System Data feed — January 2024, downloaded directly from Citi Bike's S3 bucket at analysis time, no data committed to the repo. Before anything gets counted, every pipeline in this project (the notebook and both visualizations below) applies the same cleaning rules: drop exact duplicate rides, drop trips that spill outside the claimed month at the file's edges, drop trips with non-positive duration, and drop trips over 24 hours — Citi Bike's own definition of a lost/stolen bike report, not a real ride.

The bike lane network layered into the map below is pulled live from NYC DOT's New York City Bike Routes dataset on NYC Open Data — a separate dataset from Citi Bike's own trip/station data, included to show ridership against the infrastructure it rides on.

  • CitiBike_Analysis.ipynb — the pandas/matplotlib/seaborn exploratory analysis this page is built on.
  • scripts/ — the pipeline that turns a month of raw trip data into everything on this page.
  • Both charts below are plain HTML/CSS/JS; the map uses Leaflet for the basemap and heat layer.

04 · The report

What the data actually shows

Who's riding

Members (people with a subscription) accounted for 89% of all January 2024 trips; casual riders (single-ride or day-pass) made up the other 11%. Electric bikes outnumbered classic bikes system-wide (64% vs. 36%). Trip length tells the more interesting story: the average member trip lasted 10.2 minutes, while the average casual trip ran 15.5 minutes — about 52% longer. That gap is consistent with members using the system for direct, point-to-point commuting and casual riders using it more for leisure, where the destination matters less than the ride itself.

When they ride

The heatmap above makes the weekday commute pattern hard to miss: a sharp double-peak at 8am and 5–6pm, Monday through Friday. Weekends look nothing like that — ridership spreads into one broad window centered on midday instead of two rush-hour spikes, which lines up with leisure riding rather than commuting. The single busiest hour in the entire month was Wednesday at 5pm, with 33,582 trips starting in that hour alone.

Where they ride

The system spans 2,128 stations, and NYC DOT's bike lane network — 23,807 mapped segments — is built out fairly evenly across all five boroughs. Actual ridership isn't: a rough geographic split puts roughly 39% of stations inside the Manhattan core, and that core alone accounts for something like three-quarters of all citywide rides. Brooklyn, Queens, and the Bronx all have real, mapped stations and meaningfully lower per-station volume — not the empty gap an earlier, more zoomed-in version of this map made it look like (see the map's own notes on that fix in the repo history).

What it adds up to

Together, the timing and the geography point at the same conclusion from two directions: Citi Bike in January 2024 was functioning largely as a commuter-first, Manhattan-centered system. Usage peaks exactly when commutes do, and concentrates exactly where the densest existing transit already is — which reads less like bike share replacing longer cross-borough trips, and more like it filling short last-mile gaps in a system that's already transit-rich. The outer boroughs are real, served, and comparatively quiet.

Ridership: Citi Bike System Data, January 2024. Bike lanes: NYC DOT's New York City Bike Routes dataset (current segments), via NYC Open Data.
